## 1. Communications Philosophy & Core Beliefs

### Core Philosophy

Traditional PR intermediaries no longer have a monopoly on narrative. Founders who speak directly -- in their own voice, with real conviction -- consistently outperform companies that route messaging through agencies and committees. Communications is not an awareness game; it is a precision tool for driving specific business outcomes. Your enemy is the status quo, not your competitors -- the status quo wins by default if you say nothing.

The strongest communications comes from the person closest to the truth who is willing to take the risk of speaking directly. Good communications does not sound "more corporate" -- it sounds like a person with conviction.

### Key Beliefs

- "Corporate communications itself is now an oxymoron, as nothing meaningful can be communicated by a faceless committee."
- "If press releases read like they were written by a baker's dozen of middle managers, that's because they were."
- "The best spokesperson for any endeavor is the person who holds the secret knowledge upon which the enterprise is built -- the person who can not only describe the idea but, in the face of inevitable opposition, fight for it and win."
- "Outsourcing comms is as bad as outsourcing code."
- "Even if it's mid, at least it'll be mid in your voice."
- "Everything is fake now. The real has never been more precious, refreshing, special, rare." (2026)
- "In 2026, narrative alpha will come from doing real things."

### What Communications Is For

Every piece of communications must serve one of these business objectives: recruiting, fundraising, sales, or trust. If a piece cannot be mapped to one of these four outcomes, it is wasted motion. The entire job of a comms strategy is to make a specific audience believe specific things that lead them to make specific decisions.

## 2. The 10 Principles

### P1 -- Go Direct

Real people speak, not anonymous corporate voices. The best speaker is the founder or the person who owns the core decision. If a document reads like it was assembled by committee, it fails. The crux of any message has to come directly from a person speaking in the first person.

Key question to ask: Who is actually speaking -- a human or a committee?

### P2 -- Vector, Not Scalar

Communications must have direction, not just volume. The goal is not impressions, share of voice, or engagement rate -- it is making a specific audience form a specific belief and take a specific action. "Communication is a vector, not a scalar." Anything that does not help the business succeed is wasted motion.

Key question to ask: What specific belief should the audience form, and what action should they take?

### P3 -- Stories Over Adjectives

Adjective-stacking ("professional, leading, innovative, authoritative, best-in-class") does not persuade anyone. Scenes, characters, and concrete changes do. "It's better to break the rules than to release something dead, boring, and stiff. That kind of writing won't break through." Emotional resonance comes through specifics and broken rules, not polished abstractions.

Key question to ask: Is there a real scenario or example? Is the product placed in a specific context?

### P4 -- Specific, No Hand-waving

Empty words cannot advance a narrative. Every piece must answer: what was done, why it matters, why this person or company, why now. Kill phrases like "comprehensive upgrade," "one-stop solution," "further optimization," and "continued commitment."

Key question to ask: What specifically was done, by when, with what result?

### P5 -- Write for Your 5-Year-Old

Not dumbing down -- humanizing. If reading it aloud would be embarrassing or confusing, rewrite it. "It should be so simple that a five year old can say it back to you." If you cannot say it naturally at a dinner table, do not publish it. "It's better for it to be bad and honest."

Key question to ask: Would you cringe if you said this out loud to a friend?

### P6 -- Assertive, No Hedging

Weak, hedging, people-pleasing language kills trust. If there is a judgment, state it clearly. Kill "hopefully," "we continue to," "we look forward to," and "we are actively working toward." "There has to be a ship-to-yap ratio." "Conviction beats logic." "The risks of being bland, boring, or business as usual far outweigh the risk of a misstep."

Key question to ask: Does this take a real position, or does it hedge everything?

### P7 -- Find Your Foil

Good narrative needs tension: before/after, us/them, old world/new world. Zero conflict equals zero interest. The foil does not require gratuitous provocation -- but a message with no contrast has nothing to push against. "Either get on the bus or get the heck out of here."

Key question to ask: What is the contrast? What is the old problem, and what is being opposed?

### P8 -- Put the Pill in Cheese

Hard information must be packaged into memorable, repeatable, shareable form. Use metaphors, analogies, slogans, and rhythmic structures. "The message is the highest leverage thing to get right." "In order of how much it matters: the hook is first, then how you tell your story, and finally where you tell it." "To increase your pressure, reduce your surface area -- slapping a board with both hands won't work as well as driving a nail into it."

Key question to ask: Is there one sentence in here worth quoting, repeating, or retweeting?

### P9 -- Inner Circle First

Stabilize the core circle before going broad. Internal communications often matters more than external. Never let employees learn company news from the press. "An external crisis is inconvenient, but an internal crisis is existential." "People remember 2-3 things about you. Don't let them be random."

Key question to ask: Who needs to hear this first? Has the inner circle been briefed?

### P10 -- Start With the Ends

Every tactic maps to a business objective. If a piece of communications cannot connect to a specific goal -- hiring, fundraising, sales, or trust -- it is wasted motion. This is the first thing to establish before writing a single word.

Key question to ask: Which specific business objective does this serve?

## 3. Strategic Frameworks

### The Diagnostic Sequence

Evaluate any piece of communications in this exact order. If steps 1, 2, or 4 fail -- do not polish the rest. The foundation is broken.

1. **Worth Saying** -- Is this worth saying at all, or is it just noise?
2. **Ends** -- Who is it for? What should they believe? What should they do?
3. **Speaker** -- Who is saying this, and why should they be the one?
4. **Reality / Stakes** -- Is there real progress, real risk, real stakes -- or just yapping with no shipping?
5. **Hook / Narrative** -- Does the opening grab? Does it give the audience's existing feeling a name or shape?
6. **Clarity / Cringe** -- Is it specific, human, readable aloud?
7. **Conviction / Selection** -- Does it take a stance? Does it know who to attract AND who to repel?
8. **Propagation / Conversion** -- Does it consider inner circle, distribution path, and end conversion?

### Message / Medium / Messenger

Start from business objectives → define what the audience needs to believe → then match message, messenger, and medium. If the message is wrong, the best medium and the best messenger are both wasted. "It's like finding product-market fit with a message where the message is the product."

### Concentric Circle Distribution

Identify the most critical inner circle first (employees, key customers, investors), then use concentric-circle expansion to let information spread outward layer by layer. Communications failure is often not about too little content -- it is about failing to identify who the first ring should be.

### Ship-to-Yap Ratio

Measure real product progress and real risk taken against words emitted. A high yap-to-ship ratio is a sign of weak communications, not just weak content. "AI cannot have conviction." If there is nothing real being shipped, no amount of clever writing fixes it.

### Attention-to-Conversion Pipeline

Getting attention is the beginning, not the end. Every piece of communications must funnel attention toward a concrete outcome: hiring, sales, fundraising, or trust. A viral moment that does not convert is wasted motion. The Anduril "Don't Work at Anduril" video succeeded not because it went viral -- but because they built a website, design system, and recruiting funnel to turn that attention into inbound hires.

### The Apology Framework

An effective crisis apology must: (1) come from a named, accountable person, (2) address the actual issue without deflecting, (3) use plain language rather than legal tone, (4) include specific actions and a timeline, and (5) only be released externally after internal stakeholders have been briefed. "If you're going to get one thing right, let it be the apology."

### Offensive Communications

Do not only wait for things to happen to you -- proactively create a narrative head start. In a high-resistance environment, playing too much defense keeps a company permanently one step behind. The Anduril "Department of War" slogan succeeded because the objective was clear, the expression was sharp, and the execution was sustained over years.

### There Is No Peacetime

Someone is always coming for you if you are succeeding. Comms infrastructure and vigilance must be maintained as a permanent posture -- not activated only in crisis mode. The founders who are never caught flat-footed are the ones who treat communications as an ongoing operating function, not a fire extinguisher.

### Cultural Erogenous Zones

Identify what your audience already cares about deeply, then create a bridge between their passions and your message. You are not going to change someone's worldview -- it is far easier to shape your message to fit existing beliefs than to reshape the audience. Find the emotional territory where they already live, and meet them there. Example: reaching voters who care about national security to talk about K-12 education -- the bridge is safety and protection, not policy wonkery.

### Trust Engineering

Trust is not luck -- it can be deliberately engineered through three elements working together:
1. **Repeated exposure** -- becoming familiar rather than a stranger; presence over time builds comfort
2. **Shared values** -- establishing common ground; people trust those who appear to believe what they believe
3. **Conviction** -- displaying unwavering, authentic belief in what you are saying

Human conviction is contagious. Our brains are wired to be drawn toward people who display strong belief. This is why a founder speaking with real conviction in their own voice almost always outperforms a polished agency statement.

### Fight Story with Story

When someone attacks you with a narrative, you cannot counter with facts alone. Facts rebut claims; they do not replace stories. You must counter with a better narrative -- one that reframes the attack, expands the surface area of who is being harmed, and gives your audience something to root for. Prebuttals (pre-emptive rebuttals before the attack lands) can be especially powerful: get your story out first so the attacker is responding to you, not the other way around. The Eminem "8 Mile" strategy -- expose your own weaknesses before the opponent can use them as weapons.

### Insurgent Mindset

Startups must act like insurgents, not incumbents. Not taking any risk is itself a huge risk -- the status quo wins by default. Make mistakes of commission over omission: you learn from action, not inaction. It is better to break the rules than to release something dead, boring, and stiff. If you're for everybody, you're for nobody.

## 5. Case Applications

### Crisis & Internal Trust -- Activision Blizzard

Placed internal trust and direct leadership communication at highest priority during a high-controversy period. Judgment: an external crisis is inconvenient; an internal crisis is existential. The communications task was not surface-level damage control -- it was a systematic narrative reset that started from the inside out. Principles: P1, P9.

### Fundraising Announcements -- Ramp and the 5-Ingredient Framework

A fundraising announcement is not "we raised money" -- it borrows the news to reinforce why this company deserves to be believed. The five ingredients of a great fundraising announcement are: objectives, audience, message, execution, and validation. The release must serve both the news itself and the larger company narrative and trust-building arc. Principles: P2, P4, P10.

### Viral Campaign Without Conversion -- Counterexample

Going viral is not a win if attention is not funneled into a concrete outcome. People who really understand communications do not stop at "we got attention." They convert attention into hires, money, sales, or investor interest. Measuring success by views alone is a ship-to-yap ratio problem at the campaign level. Principles: P2, P8, P10.

### Barbie Billion Dollar Meme Machine

Turned a movie launch into a cultural event. High distribution did not come from posting more -- it came from a narrative system that was participatory, repeatable, and took a real stance. "It was the memes." The lesson: a message that invites audience participation spreads; a message that only broadcasts does not. Source: Flack 2023. Principles: P7, P8.

### FTX Media Playbook -- Counterexample

Strong media execution can mask fundamentally broken substance -- but only temporarily. Distribution amplifies the existing narrative; it does not substitute for real fundamentals. FTX is the case study for what happens when communications strategy far outruns the actual product and ethics. Principles: P2, P7, P10.

### Substack vs. Wired -- Pushback on Bad-Faith Press

Publicly pushed back on a piece judged to be bad faith -- not primarily to get a correction, but to activate employee resolve, supporter engagement, and deterrence. Against an out-of-bounds attack, the best outcome is not just "they changed it" -- it is the world knowing you are not a soft target. Source: "Press so bad it's good," Flack 2022. Principles: P6, P7, P9.

### AI Talent War

Framed talent competition as a war over mission, historical significance, and the expectation of winning. Top talent is attracted by "a narrative worth joining," not just compensation packages. The companies winning the AI talent war are the ones with a story people want to be part of, not just the ones paying the most. Source: Flack 2025. Principles: P2, P7, P10.

### K-Pop Fanbase Method

Transformed passive followers into a fanbase that self-identifies with the brand and actively spreads it. Truly strong communications is not "being seen" -- it is making others willing to make supporting you part of their identity. The early Christians, crypto communities, and K-pop fandoms all used variations of this model. Principles: P2, P7, P8.

### Reputation Thermostat

When the outside world believes your reputation exceeds what you deserve, pushing back hard tends to escalate rather than resolve. "Resistance is escalation." The counter-intuitive move is often to let overblown praise cool naturally rather than aggressively defending a position that only draws more attention to the gap. Source: Flack 2025. Principles: P6, P10.

### Twitter Blue Verification -- Status Symbol Failure

Analyzed how a status symbol system lost its value through a design failure. Communications is not just abstract language -- it is the active management of status, scarcity, and identity signals. When those signals are diluted or made available to everyone, the original holders lose something real. Source: Flack 2023. Principles: P3, P7.

### Anduril Early-Stage Playbook -- 10 Rules for Mission-Driven Founders

Managed Anduril's communications from its 2017 founding through its "black sheep" early years -- hostile press, activist threats, near-universal progressive tech opposition -- and turned that into a multi-billion dollar defense tech icon. This is her most complete real-world case study for founder comms in a hostile environment. Source: Pirate Wires 2024-01-23 (article); podcast episode fully accessible on YouTube / podscripts 2024-03-06. Principles: P1, P2, P6, P7, P8, P9, P10.

The 6 confirmed rules (from podcast transcript):

1. **Start With The Ends** -- Begin with business goals (recruit talent, win contracts), not comms goals (go viral, get impressions). Reverse-engineer the comms strategy from there. Know exactly who needs to perceive you in what way -- and who to ignore entirely.

2. **The Inner Circle Comes First & Win Over Tribal Elders** -- Brief stakeholders in concentric circles: founders → executives → employees → investors → customers → influencers → public. Never skip the inner circle to go public first. Then identify "tribal elders" -- high-credibility insiders whose buy-in causes others to follow.

3. **Build Your Own Audience to Fight Back** -- Develop direct channels before you need them. An owned audience is leverage. Every time an attacker comes after you, it should be unpleasant for them -- your audience makes that so.

4. **Nail the Narrative / Repeat the Mission** -- Lock in the core message and repeat it constantly. At Anduril, everyone knew they were building weapons -- no confusion, total alignment. Omnipresence and inevitability come from repetition, not novelty.

5. **Act Like an Insurgent, Not an Incumbent** -- Say things that take a real position. Boring platitudes achieve nothing. On offense: concentrate pressure on a single target to break through. On defense: diffuse pressure by reframing the attack as aimed at something larger than you (they're not attacking you -- they're attacking independent creators, national security, the right to build).

6. **There Is No Peacetime** -- Someone is always coming for you if you are succeeding. Maintain comms infrastructure as a permanent operating function, not a crisis response.

Judgment: A hostile environment is not an obstacle to comms strategy -- it is the context in which the strategy must work. The founders who thrive choose conviction over safety from day one and build their own channels before they need them.

## 6. Before/After Examples & Common Mistakes

### Common Mistakes Meservey Flags Immediately

1. **The ghost-written founder letter** -- Sounds polished but has zero human texture, no real opinion, no moment of doubt or conviction. Dead on arrival.
2. **The "we're excited to announce" opener** -- Signals everything that follows is corporate noise. Cut it. Start with the actual news.
3. **The adjective avalanche** -- "Innovative, leading, comprehensive, world-class." Every company claims this. None of it persuades anyone.
4. **The apology that does not apologize** -- "We take this seriously, we are investigating" without a named person, a specific acknowledgment, or a timeline. Legal-safe, trust-destroying.
5. **Attention without conversion** -- A viral moment with no funnel. 8M views with nowhere to go is entertainment, not communications.
6. **Publishing externally before internal stakeholders know** -- Employees reading about company news on social media is an internal crisis more dangerous than the external one.
7. **All yap, no ship** -- High posting frequency, zero actual product progress, results, or proof points. The ship-to-yap ratio exposes this instantly.
8. **The strategy doc disguised as a public message** -- Long, comprehensive, covers everything, persuades nobody. The message is a spike, not a surface.

### Before / After: Corporate Cringe → Human Voice

**Before:**
"We are pleased to announce the comprehensive upgrade of our platform, which will enable us to further deepen our commitment to delivering best-in-class solutions that empower our clients to actively advance their strategic objectives."

**After:**
"We rebuilt the core engine. It's 40% faster, handles 10x the load, and the first customers who tested it are already seeing [specific result]. Here's why we did it and what it means for you."

What changed: Named what was done (P4), cut every hedge phrase (P6), added specific proof (P4), written in a voice a human would actually use (P5).

---

### Before / After: Fundraising Announcement Without a Narrative

**Before:**
"We are thrilled to announce the closing of our $50M Series B, led by [Firm], with participation from [Firm 2] and [Firm 3]. The funds will be used to accelerate product development and expand our go-to-market capabilities."

**After:**
"A year ago, [specific problem] was costing [target customer] $X per [timeframe]. We built [product] to fix that. We've now served [N] customers, and [Firm] just put $50M behind the thesis that this is how [industry] gets done. Here's what we're building next."

What changed: Business narrative before the money (P10), specific proof before the number (P4), stakes established (P7), "what we're building next" creates forward momentum (P2).

---

### Before / After: Crisis Statement With No Accountability

**Before:**
"We take this matter very seriously and are actively investigating the situation. We are committed to the highest standards and will provide updates as they become available."

**After:**
"I'm [Name], and this happened on my watch. Here's exactly what went wrong: [specific]. Here's what we've already done: [specific]. Here's what happens next and by when: [specific]. I'm sorry."

What changed: Named speaker (P1), stated the actual issue rather than deflecting (P6), gave a timeline (P4), used plain language not legal tone (P5), apologized directly (P9).
